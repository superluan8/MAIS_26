Syllabus Overview

All 5 topics — hover any card to explore

TOPIC 1

Number & Algebra

Arithmetic & geometric sequences, compound interest, annuities, loans, amortisation, exponents, logarithms, and scientific notation.

Sequences Finance Logarithms
Sequences
  • Arithmetic sequences increase or decrease by a fixed number called the common difference (d). Example: 3, 7, 11, 15… (d = 4)
  • Geometric sequences multiply by a fixed number each time, called the common ratio (r). Example: 2, 6, 18, 54… (r = 3)
  • The sum of a sequence (S_n) adds up all terms from u₁ to u_n — memorise both sum formulas.
Financial Mathematics
  • Compound interest means you earn interest on your interest — money grows exponentially over time.
  • Annuities are regular equal payments (e.g. monthly savings into an account). Your GDC TVM solver handles all finance questions.
  • Amortisation is the process of paying off a loan gradually — each payment covers interest first, then reduces the principal.
Logarithms & Exponents
  • Logarithms are the inverse of exponentials: if aˣ = b, then log_a(b) = x. Think of it as "what power do I raise a to, to get b?"
  • Key log laws: log(AB) = log A + log B  |  log(A/B) = log A − log B  |  log(Aⁿ) = n·log A
⚠️ Common mistake: mixing up arithmetic vs geometric. Ask yourself — are terms being added to, or multiplied by, a fixed value?
TOPIC 2

Functions

Linear, quadratic, exponential, sinusoidal, and piecewise models. Interpreting graphs, inverse functions, and real-world modelling.

Modelling Graphs Transformations
Types of Functions
  • Linear f(x) = mx + c — straight line. m = gradient, c = y-intercept. Used for constant rate of change.
  • Quadratic f(x) = ax² + bx + c — parabola. Has a vertex (turning point), axis of symmetry, and at most 2 x-intercepts.
  • Exponential f(x) = a·bˣ + c — models growth (b > 1) or decay (0 < b < 1). Has a horizontal asymptote at y = c.
  • Sinusoidal f(x) = a·sin(b(x−c)) + d — models repeating/periodic patterns like tides and temperature cycles.
Key Graph Features
  • Domain = all possible x-values (inputs). Range = all possible y-values (outputs).
  • x-intercepts / zeros: where the graph crosses the x-axis (y = 0). y-intercept: where it crosses y-axis (x = 0).
  • Asymptotes are invisible lines the graph approaches but never touches — important for exponential models.
Transformations
  • f(x) + k → vertical shift up by k. f(x − h) → horizontal shift right by h.
  • a·f(x) → vertical stretch. f(bx) → horizontal compression.
💡 In AI SL the focus is always on choosing the right model for a context and interpreting the parameters — not just calculating them.
TOPIC 3

Geometry & Trigonometry

Voronoi diagrams, right-angle and non-right trig, sine/cosine rule, area formula, volume and surface area of 3D shapes.

Trig Voronoi 3D Shapes
Right-Angle Trigonometry
  • SOH-CAH-TOA: sin θ = Opposite/Hypotenuse, cos θ = Adjacent/Hypotenuse, tan θ = Opposite/Adjacent.
  • Use when you have a right angle + one side + one angle (or two sides to find an angle).
  • Pythagoras a² + b² = c² finds the missing side when you have two sides of a right triangle.
Non-Right Trigonometry
  • Sine Rule — use when you have a matching angle-side pair (AAS or ASA situations).
  • Cosine Rule — use when you have three sides (SSS) or two sides + included angle (SAS).
  • Area = ½ab·sinC — when you know two sides and the angle between them.
3D Shapes & Voronoi
  • You need to know volume and surface area formulas for prisms, cylinders, cones, spheres, and pyramids — most are in the formula booklet.
  • Voronoi diagrams divide a plane into regions based on which point (site) is nearest. The boundaries are perpendicular bisectors of the lines joining sites.
  • Adding a new site to a Voronoi diagram requires finding the new perpendicular bisectors and adjusting the regions.
⚠️ Always check your GDC is in DEGREE mode for all trig problems unless told otherwise. Radian answers will be completely wrong.
TOPIC 4

Statistics & Probability

Data collection, measures of central tendency & spread, correlation, regression, normal distribution, binomial distribution, hypothesis testing.

Normal Dist. Regression Hypothesis
Descriptive Statistics
  • Mean (average), median (middle value), mode (most frequent) are measures of central tendency.
  • Standard deviation (σ) measures how spread out data is from the mean. IQR = Q3 − Q1, the range of the middle 50%.
  • Outliers are values more than 1.5 × IQR beyond Q1 or Q3 — always check for them on box plots.
Correlation & Regression
  • Pearson's r measures the strength and direction of a linear relationship. Calculated by GDC.
  • The regression line y = ax + b is the line of best fit. Only use it to predict values within the data range (interpolation, not extrapolation).
  • Correlation does not imply causation — two things can be correlated without one causing the other.
Probability Distributions
  • Normal distribution X ~ N(μ, σ²) — bell-shaped, symmetric about the mean. Used for continuous data (heights, weights, test scores).
  • Binomial distribution X ~ B(n, p) — counts how many successes in n independent trials, each with probability p.
  • For binomial: P(X = k) uses the formula. Your GDC calculates binomPDF (exact) and binomCDF (cumulative).
Hypothesis Testing
  • The null hypothesis H₀ states "no effect / no difference." The alternative H₁ states there is a difference.
  • The p-value is the probability of getting results at least as extreme as observed, assuming H₀ is true.
  • If p-value < significance level (usually 0.05), reject H₀. If p-value ≥ 0.05, do not reject H₀.
⚠️ Never say "accept H₀" — the correct phrase is "there is insufficient evidence to reject H₀."
TOPIC 5

Calculus

Differentiation, integration, optimisation, area under curves, kinematics, and trapezoidal rule — all in real-world contexts.

Differentiation Integration Optimisation
Differentiation
  • The derivative f′(x) gives the gradient (slope) of the curve at any point x. It measures the rate of change.
  • Power rule: differentiate xⁿ → nxⁿ⁻¹. Apply term by term for polynomials.
  • Setting f′(x) = 0 finds turning points (maxima or minima). Use f″(x) to determine which: f″ > 0 → min, f″ < 0 → max.
  • In context: if s(t) is position, then s′(t) = velocity and s″(t) = acceleration.
Integration
  • Integration is the reverse of differentiation. The integral of xⁿ = xⁿ⁺¹/(n+1) + c.
  • A definite integral ∫ₐᵇ f(x) dx calculates the signed area between the curve and the x-axis between x = a and x = b.
  • For area between two curves: integrate (top function − bottom function) over the interval.
Optimisation & Trapezoidal Rule
  • Optimisation: write an expression for what you want to maximise/minimise, differentiate, set = 0, solve, and verify it's a max/min.
  • The trapezoidal rule approximates area under a curve by dividing it into trapezoids: A ≈ h/2 × (y₀ + 2y₁ + 2y₂ + … + yₙ), where h = strip width.
💡 Calculus in AI SL is always applied to real-world scenarios — profit, volume, speed. Always interpret your answer in context with units.
⚠️ Don't forget +c for indefinite integrals. For definite integrals, area below the x-axis gives a negative value — if you need physical area, take the absolute value.
💡 Exam tip: Topics 4 (Statistics) and 3 (Geometry) carry the most marks in Paper 2. Prioritise your GDC skills here.

Key Formulas

All formulas from the official IB booklet — rendered in LaTeX with plain-English explanations

1.2 · Arithmetic Sequence — nth Term
\(u_n = u_1 + (n-1)d\)
Finds the nth term of an arithmetic sequence, where each term increases or decreases by the fixed common difference \(d\).
\(u_1\) = first term · \(d\) = common difference · \(n\) = term number
⚡ Find \(d = u_2 - u_1\). To find which term equals a value, set \(u_n =\) value and solve for \(n\).
1.2 · Arithmetic Sequence — Sum
\(S_n = \dfrac{n}{2}\bigl(2u_1 + (n-1)d\bigr) = \dfrac{n}{2}(u_1 + u_n)\)
Adds up the first \(n\) terms. The second form is handy when you already know the last term \(u_n\) — it's just \(n\) × average of first and last.
\(u_1\) = first term · \(u_n\) = last term · \(d\) = common difference
⚡ Both forms give the same answer — use whichever has fewer unknowns to substitute.
1.3 · Geometric Sequence — nth Term
\(u_n = u_1 \cdot r^{\,n-1}\)
Finds the nth term of a geometric sequence. Each term is multiplied by the common ratio \(r\).
\(u_1\) = first term · \(r = u_2/u_1\) = common ratio
⚡ \(r>1\) = growth · \(0 < r < 1\) = decay · \(r < 0\) = alternating signs.
1.3 · Geometric Sequence — Sum
\(S_n = \dfrac{u_1(r^n-1)}{r-1} = \dfrac{u_1(1-r^n)}{1-r}, \quad r \neq 1\)
Adds up a finite geometric sequence. Both forms are equivalent — use the one that avoids a negative denominator.
If \(r = 1\), all terms are equal so \(S_n = n \cdot u_1\)
⚡ This counts a finite number of terms only — don't confuse with an infinite series.
1.4 · Compound Interest
\(FV = PV \times \left(1 + \dfrac{r}{100k}\right)^{kn}\)
Calculates the future value of an investment. Interest is added to the principal at each period — you earn interest on interest, giving exponential growth.
\(PV\) = present value · \(n\) = years · \(k\) = periods/year · \(r\%\) = nominal annual rate
⚡ Monthly: \(k=12\), quarterly: \(k=4\), annually: \(k=1\). Use GDC TVM solver for loans and annuities.
1.5 · Exponents & Logarithms
\(\log_a x = b \iff a^b = x \quad (a>0,\; a\neq1,\; x>0)\)
A log answers: "what power do I raise \(a\) to in order to get \(x\)?" Logs and exponents are inverse operations. E.g. \(\log_2 8 = 3\) because \(2^3=8\).
\(\log_{10} x = \log x\) (common log) · \(\log_e x = \ln x\) (natural log)
⚡ Key laws: \(\log(AB)=\log A+\log B\) · \(\log\tfrac{A}{B}=\log A-\log B\) · \(\log A^n = n\log A\)
1.6 · Percentage Error
\(\varepsilon = \left|\dfrac{v_E - v_A}{v_E}\right| \times 100\%\)
Measures how far off an approximation is as a percentage of the true (exact) value. The absolute value ensures the result is always non-negative.
\(v_E\) = exact value · \(v_A\) = approximate value
⚡ Always divide by the exact value, not the approximate one. Smaller % = better approximation.
2.1 · Equations of a Straight Line
\(y = mx + c \qquad ax+by+d=0 \qquad y-y_1=m(x-x_1)\)
Three equivalent forms. Slope-intercept is most readable; point-slope is quickest when you have a point and gradient; general form appears in some exam questions.
\(m\) = gradient · \(c\) = y-intercept · \((x_1,y_1)\) = a known point on the line
⚡ Parallel lines share the same \(m\). Perpendicular: \(m_1 \times m_2 = -1\).
2.1 · Gradient Formula
\(m = \dfrac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}\)
"Rise over run" — how much \(y\) changes per unit increase in \(x\). Positive = rises left-to-right; negative = falls left-to-right.
\((x_1,y_1)\) and \((x_2,y_2)\) = any two distinct points on the line
⚡ Order doesn't matter as long as you're consistent — use the same point's coordinates on both top and bottom.
2.5 · Axis of Symmetry (Quadratic)
\(f(x)=ax^2+bx+c \implies x = -\dfrac{b}{2a}\)
A parabola is symmetric about its axis of symmetry through the vertex. Substitute this \(x\) back into \(f(x)\) to find the max or min value.
\(a,b,c\) = quadratic coefficients · \(a \neq 0\)
⚡ \(a>0\) → opens upward (min vertex). \(a<0\) → opens downward (max vertex).
Exponential Model · Functions
\(f(t) = a \cdot b^{\,t} + c\)
Models exponential growth or decay. The horizontal asymptote \(y=c\) is a value the function approaches but never crosses.
\(a\) = scale factor · \(b>1\) growth / \(0<b<1\) decay · \(c\) = asymptote
⚡ Population growth, radioactive decay, Newton's cooling — all exponential. Find \(b\) from two data points.
Sinusoidal Model · Functions
\(f(x) = a\sin\!\bigl(b(x-c)\bigr) + d\)
Models periodic / wave data like tides, temperature, and Ferris wheels. Every parameter has a clear geometric meaning readable from the graph.
\(a\)= amplitude · period \(=2\pi/b\) · \(c\)= horizontal shift · \(d\)= midline
⚡ Amplitude \(=\tfrac{\max-\min}{2}\) · Midline \(=\tfrac{\max+\min}{2}\). Read these from context first!
Prior Learning · Basic Areas
\(A_\triangle = \tfrac{1}{2}bh \quad A_{\parallel} = bh \quad A_\text{trap} = \tfrac{1}{2}(a+b)h \quad A_\circ = \pi r^2\)
Core area formulas. The trapezoid formula averages the two parallel sides then multiplies by height. Always use the perpendicular height.
\(b\)= base · \(h\)= perpendicular height · \(a,b\)= parallel sides · \(r\)= radius
Prior Learning · Volumes & Circumference
\(V_\text{cuboid}=lwh \quad V_\text{cyl}=\pi r^2 h \quad V_\text{prism}=Ah \quad C=2\pi r\)
For any prism, volume = cross-sectional area × height. The cylinder is just a circular prism: \(A = \pi r^2\).
\(A\)= cross-sectional area · \(l,w,h\)= length, width, height · \(r\)= radius
Prior Learning · Distance & Midpoint (2D)
\(d=\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2} \qquad M=\!\left(\dfrac{x_1+x_2}{2},\,\dfrac{y_1+y_2}{2}\right)\)
Distance is Pythagoras in disguise. Midpoint is simply the average of both coordinates — the point exactly halfway between.
\((x_1,y_1)\) and \((x_2,y_2)\)= two points in the plane
3.1 · Distance & Midpoint (3D)
\(d=\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2+(z_2-z_1)^2}\)
Extends Pythagoras to three dimensions by adding the \(z\) component under the square root. Midpoint is still the coordinate-wise average: \(\left(\frac{x_1+x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1+y_2}{2}, \frac{z_1+z_2}{2}\right)\).
\((x_1,y_1,z_1)\) and \((x_2,y_2,z_2)\)= two points in 3D space
3.1 · Pyramid, Cone & Sphere Volumes
\(V_\text{pyr}=\tfrac{1}{3}Ah \quad V_\text{cone}=\tfrac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h \quad V_\text{sphere}=\tfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3\)
These solids all include the factor \(\tfrac{1}{3}\) compared to their prism equivalents. The sphere formula is one of the most important to memorise.
\(A\)= base area · \(h\)= perpendicular height · \(r\)= radius
⚡ Pyramid volume = \(\tfrac{1}{3}\) × (base area × height) — works for any base shape.
3.1 · Curved Surface Areas
\(A_\text{cone}=\pi rl \quad A_\text{cyl (curved)}=2\pi rh \quad A_\text{sphere}=4\pi r^2\)
These are curved surfaces only — add \(\pi r^2\) base(s) for closed solids. Note the sphere's surface area is exactly 4× the area of a great circle.
\(r\)= radius · \(l\)= slant height (cone) · \(h\)= height (cylinder)
⚡ Slant height \(l \neq\) vertical height \(h\). Use Pythagoras: \(l = \sqrt{r^2+h^2}\).
3.2 · Sine Rule
\(\dfrac{a}{\sin A} = \dfrac{b}{\sin B} = \dfrac{c}{\sin C}\)
Use when you have an angle–opposite side pair and need another side or angle. Works for any triangle — not just right-angled.
\(a,b,c\)= sides · \(A,B,C\)= angles opposite those sides
⚡ Ambiguous case (SSA): two triangles may exist. Always check if a second solution is valid.
3.2 · Cosine Rule
\(c^2 = a^2+b^2-2ab\cos C \qquad \cos C = \dfrac{a^2+b^2-c^2}{2ab}\)
Use with 3 sides (find angle) or 2 sides + included angle (find 3rd side). It's a generalisation of Pythagoras — when \(C=90°\) the last term vanishes.
\(C\)= angle between sides \(a\) and \(b\) · \(c\)= side opposite \(C\)
⚡ Make sure GDC is in DEGREE mode. Draw a labelled diagram first.
3.2 · Area of Triangle (Non-right)
\(A = \dfrac{1}{2}\,ab\sin C\)
Area of any triangle when you know two sides and the included angle. When \(C=90°\), \(\sin 90°=1\) and it reduces to the familiar \(\tfrac{1}{2}bh\).
\(a,b\)= two known sides · \(C\)= the angle between them (included angle)
3.4 · Arc Length & Sector Area
\(l = \dfrac{\theta}{360} \times 2\pi r \qquad A = \dfrac{\theta}{360} \times \pi r^2\)
A sector is a "pizza slice". The fraction \(\theta/360\) scales the full circumference or area to just the portion you need. These use degrees as in the IB booklet.
\(\theta\)= central angle in degrees · \(r\)= radius · \(l\)= arc length
⚡ Check: \(\theta=360°\) gives \(l=2\pi r\) and \(A=\pi r^2\). ✓
4.2 · Interquartile Range
\(\text{IQR} = Q_3 - Q_1\)
Measures the spread of the middle 50% of data. Resistant to outliers because it ignores the top and bottom quarters. Larger IQR = more spread.
\(Q_1\)= lower quartile (25th percentile) · \(Q_3\)= upper quartile (75th percentile)
⚡ Outlier rule: value is an outlier if below \(Q_1 - 1.5\times\text{IQR}\) or above \(Q_3 + 1.5\times\text{IQR}\).
4.3 · Mean of Frequency Data
\(\bar{x} = \dfrac{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{k} f_i x_i}{n}, \qquad n = \sum_{i=1}^{k} f_i\)
Weighted mean from a frequency table. Each value \(x_i\) is multiplied by how often it appears \(f_i\), summed, then divided by total count \(n\).
\(x_i\)= data value · \(f_i\)= its frequency · \(n\)= total data count
⚡ For grouped data, use the class midpoint as \(x_i\). GDC handles this in the statistics list.
4.5 · Probability & Complement
\(P(A) = \dfrac{n(A)}{n(U)} \qquad P(A) + P(A') = 1\)
Classical probability: favourable outcomes ÷ total equally-likely outcomes. The complement rule says the probability of something not happening = 1 − probability it does.
\(n(A)\)= outcomes in event \(A\) · \(n(U)\)= total outcomes · \(A'\)= complement of \(A\)
⚡ All probabilities across a complete set of outcomes must sum to 1.
4.6 · Combined Events (Union Rule)
\(P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)\)
Adds both event probabilities then subtracts the overlap to avoid double-counting. If \(A\) and \(B\) are mutually exclusive (no overlap), then \(P(A \cap B)=0\) so \(P(A \cup B)=P(A)+P(B)\).
\(A \cup B\)= A or B (or both) · \(A \cap B\)= A and B simultaneously
⚡ Use a Venn diagram or two-way table to organise intersections clearly.
4.6 · Conditional Probability & Independence
\(P(A|B) = \dfrac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)} \qquad P(A \cap B)=P(A)\,P(B) \text{ if independent}\)
Conditional restricts the sample space to cases where \(B\) has already occurred. Independent means knowing \(B\) doesn't change the probability of \(A\).
\(P(A|B)\)= probability of A given B has occurred
⚡ Tree diagrams are ideal for conditional probability. Multiply along branches, add between branches.
4.7 · Expected Value (Discrete)
\(E(X) = \displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{k} x_i \cdot P(X = x_i)\)
The long-run average if the experiment were repeated many times. It's a weighted average of all outcomes by their probabilities. It may not be a value \(X\) can actually take.
\(x_i\)= possible value · \(P(X=x_i)\)= its probability
⚡ Always verify: all probabilities in the distribution must sum to exactly 1.
4.8 · Binomial Distribution
\(X \sim B(n,\,p) \qquad E(X)=np \qquad \text{Var}(X)=np(1-p)\)
Counts successes in \(n\) independent trials, each with the same probability \(p\). The mean \(np\) is the expected number of successes.
\(n\)= number of trials · \(p\)= probability of success per trial
⚡ GDC: binomPDF\((n,p,k)\) for \(P(X=k)\). binomCDF\((n,p,k)\) for \(P(X \leq k)\). Check: fixed \(n\), independent trials, constant \(p\).
Normal Distribution · Statistics
\(X \sim N(\mu,\;\sigma^2)\)
Bell-curve model for continuous data. \(\mu\) is the mean (centre of symmetry), \(\sigma\) is the standard deviation (spread). About 68% of data falls within 1\(\sigma\), 95% within 2\(\sigma\).
\(\mu\)= mean · \(\sigma^2\)= variance · \(\sigma\)= standard deviation
⚡ GDC: normalcdf\((a,b,\mu,\sigma)\) for probabilities. invNorm\((p,\mu,\sigma)\) to find x-values. Use 9E999 for \(+\infty\).
5.3 · Power Rule — Differentiation
\(f(x) = x^n \implies f'(x) = nx^{n-1}\)
Bring the exponent down as a coefficient, then reduce the exponent by 1. The derivative gives the gradient (slope) of the curve at any point — the instantaneous rate of change.
Works for any real \(n\), including fractions and negatives. Apply term-by-term for polynomials.
⚡ \(f'(x)=0\) → turning point · \(f''(x)>0\) → min · \(f''(x)<0\) → max. Kinematics: position → velocity → acceleration.
5.5 · Power Rule — Integration
\(\displaystyle\int x^n\,dx = \dfrac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C, \quad n \neq -1\)
Integration is the reverse of differentiation: increase exponent by 1, divide by the new exponent. The constant \(+C\) accounts for all functions sharing the same derivative (e.g. \(x^2\) and \(x^2+5\) both differentiate to \(2x\)).
\(C\)= constant of integration (required for all indefinite integrals)
⚡ Never forget \(+C\) for indefinite integrals. For definite integrals \(C\) cancels out.
5.5 · Area Under a Curve
\(A = \displaystyle\int_a^b y\;dx, \quad f(x) \geq 0\)
A definite integral calculates signed area between \(y=f(x)\) and the \(x\)-axis from \(x=a\) to \(x=b\). Area below the axis is negative — take the absolute value for physical area.
\(a,b\)= limits of integration · \(f(x)\geq0\) for area to be directly positive
⚡ Area between two curves: \(\int_a^b[f(x)-g(x)]\,dx\) where \(f\) is the top curve. GDC computes definite integrals directly.
5.8 · Trapezoidal Rule
\(\displaystyle\int_a^b y\,dx \approx \frac{h}{2}\bigl(y_0 + 2y_1 + 2y_2 + \cdots + 2y_{n-1} + y_n\bigr)\)
Approximates area under a curve by dividing it into \(n\) vertical trapezoids of equal width \(h=(b-a)/n\). First and last \(y\)-values get coefficient 1; all interior values get 2.
\(h=(b-a)/n\)= strip width · \(y_0,\ldots,y_n\)= function values at each boundary
⚡ Memory trick: coefficients are "1, 2, 2, …, 2, 1". More strips → better approximation.

IB-Style Practice Questions

Reveal solutions step by step. Mirrors real IB exam style.

EasyArithmetic Sequences[4 marks]
The first term of an arithmetic sequence is 3 and the common difference is 7.

(a) Write down the fourth term.
(b) Find the 20th term.
(c) Find the sum of the first 20 terms.

✅ Solution

  1. (a) u₄ = 3 + (4−1)×7 = 3 + 21 = 24
  2. (b) u₂₀ = 3 + 19×7 = 3 + 133 = 136
  3. (c) S₂₀ = 20/2 × (2×3 + 19×7) = 10 × 139 = 1390
🔢 Also valid: S_n = n/2(u₁ + u_n) = 10 × (3 + 136) = 1390 ✓
MediumSine & Cosine Rule[6 marks]
Triangle ABC has AB = 12 cm, BC = 9 cm, and angle ABC = 67°.

(a) Find the length of AC.
(b) Find angle BAC.
(c) Find the area of triangle ABC.

✅ Solution

  1. (a) AC² = 12² + 9² − 2(12)(9)cos67° ≈ 140.6 → AC ≈ 11.9 cm
  2. (b) sinBAC/9 = sin67°/11.9 → BAC ≈ 43.9°
  3. (c) Area = ½ × 12 × 9 × sin67° ≈ 49.8 cm²
🔢 Always draw a diagram! Make sure GDC is in DEGREE mode.
MediumNormal Distribution[5 marks]
Student heights are normally distributed with μ = 168 cm and σ = 8 cm.

(a) Find P(160 < X < 180).
(b) Find the height exceeded by 10% of students.
(c) If there are 200 students, find the expected number taller than 180 cm.

✅ Solution

  1. (a) normalcdf(160, 180, 168, 8) ≈ 0.766
  2. (b) invNorm(0.90, 168, 8) ≈ 178.3 cm
  3. (c) normalcdf(180, 9E99, 168, 8) ≈ 0.0668 → 200 × 0.0668 ≈ 13 students
HardOptimisation[7 marks]
A farmer has 120 m of fencing for a rectangular field against a straight wall (wall = one side, no fencing needed).

(a) Let x = width. Write the length in terms of x.
(b) Show that A = 120x − 2x².
(c) Find x that maximises area.
(d) Find the maximum area.

✅ Solution

  1. (a) 2x + length = 120 → length = 120 − 2x
  2. (b) A = x(120 − 2x) = 120x − 2x² ✓
  3. (c) dA/dx = 120 − 4x = 0 → x = 30 m
  4. (d) A = 120(30) − 2(900) = 3600 − 1800 = 1800 m²
🔢 Verify: plot A = 120x − 2x² on GDC and use maximum function to confirm x = 30.
